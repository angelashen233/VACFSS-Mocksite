Ways to Care

Types of Caregiving

There are several ways to become a VACFSS caregiver. Each path provides a meaningful opportunity to support Indigenous children and youth in different ways.

Foster Care

A temporary home while family healing happens

Foster caregivers provide a safe, stable home for children who cannot live with their birth family, often while parents work toward reunification with support from VACFSS.

  • Short or long-term placements
  • Children of all ages
  • Monthly financial support provided
  • Dedicated support worker assigned
Kinship Care

Keeping children connected to family and community

Kinship care places children with relatives, extended family, or close community members — preserving cultural identity, family bonds, and a sense of belonging.

  • Extended family or community members
  • Maintains cultural connections
  • Priority placement option
  • Financial and training support
Respite Care

Short-term relief for caregivers who need a break

Respite caregivers offer planned short-term care — a weekend, a week — giving primary caregivers rest while ensuring children remain in a safe, culturally appropriate setting.

  • Flexible, planned short stays
  • Great starting point for new caregivers
  • Hourly compensation provided
  • Full training and support included
Eligibility

Who Can Become a Caregiver?

VACFSS welcomes caregivers from all backgrounds. We prioritize Indigenous caregivers to keep children connected to their culture, but we welcome all who are committed to the well-being of Indigenous children.

You May Be Eligible If You…

  • Are 19 years of age or older
  • Have a safe and suitable home environment
  • Are able to provide a stable, nurturing environment
  • Have sufficient income to meet your own household needs
  • Are willing to complete required training
  • Can pass a criminal record check
  • Are committed to supporting a child's cultural identity

We Welcome…

  • Single caregivers and couples
  • Families with or without children
  • Renters and homeowners
  • Retired individuals with capacity and commitment
  • LGBTQ2S+ caregivers
  • Indigenous and non-Indigenous applicants
  • Those with lived experience in the child welfare system
Our Support

You Won't Walk This Path Alone

VACFSS provides comprehensive support to every caregiver — because when caregivers thrive, children thrive.

PRIDE Training

Comprehensive pre-service training to prepare you for the rewards and challenges of caregiving.

Financial Support

Monthly maintenance rates, clothing allowances, and additional funding for children with complex needs.

Dedicated Worker

A VACFSS support worker is assigned to you — available to guide, advocate, and assist throughout your journey.

Respite Funding

Access planned respite care so you can rest, recharge, and continue providing quality care.

Cultural Support

Resources, guidance, and programming to help you honour and nurture a child's Indigenous cultural identity.

Caregiver Community

Connect with other VACFSS caregivers through peer support groups, events, and shared experiences.

The Process

Your Path to Becoming a Caregiver

We walk with you at every stage. The process typically takes 3–6 months from your first enquiry to welcoming a child into your home.

Step 1
Enquire

Contact our Caregiver Recruitment team to ask questions and express your interest.

Step 2
Orientation

Attend an information session to learn about what caregiving involves and whether it's the right fit for your family.

Step 3
Assessment

A home study and background screening helps us understand your family, home environment, and strengths.

Step 4
Training

Complete the PRIDE training program designed to prepare you for the rewards and challenges ahead.

Step 5
Placement

We work collaboratively to match a child whose needs align with your home and family strengths.

Step 6
Ongoing Support

Dedicated workers, respite funding, training, and a community of caregivers are always there for you.

Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to be Indigenous to become a VACFSS caregiver?

No. While we prioritize Indigenous caregivers to support cultural continuity, we welcome all applicants who are committed to nurturing a child's Indigenous identity and supporting their connection to culture, language, and community.

Can I be a caregiver if I rent my home?

Yes. You do not need to own your home to become a caregiver. What matters most is that your home is safe, stable, and has adequate space for a child. A home visit is part of the assessment process.

Will I receive financial support as a caregiver?

Yes. VACFSS provides monthly maintenance rates to cover the costs of caring for a child, including food, clothing, transportation, and activities. Additional funding may be available for children with higher support needs.

What if I already have children at home?

Having children already is not a barrier. Many VACFSS caregivers have their own children at home. We assess the dynamics of your household to ensure a placement would be a positive fit for everyone involved.

How long does the application process take?

The process typically takes 3–6 months from your initial enquiry to being approved and ready for a placement. The timeline depends on how quickly documents are submitted, scheduling for training, and completion of the home study.
